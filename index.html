Dr Spencer Angus Thomas

_____________



Senior Research Scientist in Data Science at the National
Physical Laboratory and a Visiting Research Fellow at the
Department of Computer Science at the University of
Surrey. Spencer joined NPL in 2016 working on
developing deep learning and computational analysis
methods for analysing complex, high dimensional
hyperspectral imaging data, and non-imaging data,
particularly in healthcare and life science. I currently co-
supervises PhD students with Surrey in the area of digital
pathology (Uni of Surrey, Vet School and CVSSP), an
iCASE studentship on deep learning in mass spectrometry
data (Uni of Surrey, Department of Computer Science),
and an available project on machine learning in medical
device data (Uni of Edinburgh and GSK).

Prior to NPL Spencer worked as a Research Fellow in
Applied Mathematics at the University of Surrey,
developing algorithms to analyse nonlinear, stochastic and
complex systems, following his PhD on computational
intelligence in biological systems. He graduated with a
First Class Honours MPhys degree from the University of
Surrey in 2010, which included a year-long research
placement at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Astrophysics,
University of Notre Dame, Indiana.

MY LATEST RESEARCH

___________


 
                              Development of machine learning and multivariate
                              analysis techniques to understand complex data sets.
                              In particular I am interested in deep learning and its
                              application to mass spectrometer imaging data.
                              Typically these data sets are high dimensional,
                              complex, and range from gigabytes to terabytes.
              
                              Development of techniques for analysing complex
                              systems, stochastic systems and simulation based
                              models where traditional methods fail. Combining
                              emerging and well established tools to extract insight
                              from 'black box' simulations.
              
                              Uncovering drivers in the system and qualifying their
                              effects improve understanding and can identify
                              favourable outcomes. Moreover, this also provides a
                              basis for determining interventions to steer the system
                              to a desired state.
              
                              Using mathematical analysis tools we are able to
                              identify parameter dependencies, transitions
                              (bifurcations), regime shifts, and statistical analysis of
                              complex systems. The tools we are
                              developing are generic but currently our focus is to aid decision
                              making for policy around social ecological areas.
